National Festivals

National festivals are celebrated all over the world to bring communities together and honour cultural heritage. In many countries, these celebrations are massive events that require months of careful preparation. People decorate streets with colourful banners, bright lights, and traditional lanterns. Families gather to share delicious meals, exchange traditional sweets, and enjoy live music together.

One major aspect of these national celebrations is the parade. Large crowds gather along the main roads to watch dancers, musicians, and beautiful floats pass by. For many citizens, participating in or watching these events is an important experience that strengthens their sense of belonging to their country. In the United Arab Emirates, National Day is a prime example, where people enthusiastically wave flags and express great pride in their nation’s history and achievements.

While some festivals are religious, national festivals focus primarily on the history, achievements, and unity of a specific country. They provide a wonderful opportunity for visitors to learn about local traditions, clothing, and food. Many schools and businesses close for the day so that everyone can fully participate in the celebrations. These events are essential for keeping ancient cultural practices alive for younger generations who might otherwise forget them in the modern world. Without such celebrations, many important traditions could gradually disappear forever.

My African Adventure Blog — Posted by Tariq

Last month, I finally fulfilled my lifelong dream of exploring the incredible landscapes of East Africa. My journey began in Tanzania, where our expedition group set out early in the morning to explore the famous Serengeti. Travelling across the wide grasslands in an open-top vehicle was an experience I will never forget. The terrain was beautiful but dangerous, filled with wild predators hunting for their prey.

Our local guide, Juma, was an expert. He could spot a leopard hidden in a tree from a long distance. On our third day, we faced a terrible situation when our vehicle got stuck in deep mud near a river. It was a potential disaster because the sun was setting, and wild animals were active. Luckily, Juma used a strong rope and a nearby tree to pull the vehicle out safely. No one got hurt, but it was a scary reminder of how unpredictable nature can be.

Later, we visited a traditional village. The local people welcomed our crowd of travellers with bright smiles and delicious food. They performed a traditional dance around a campfire under the night sky. This trip was expensive, but the memories of the wildlife and the wonderful people I met made every single dirham worth it.

Dear Sarah,

I am writing to invite you to the Annual International Children’s Food Festival next weekend! It is being held at the City Park from Friday to Sunday. I went last year with my family, and it was a spectacular experience. This year promises to be even bigger and more exciting, and I immediately thought of you because I know how much you love trying new recipes.

The festival features rows of colourful stalls representing different countries. Chefs from around the world prepare miniature versions of their traditional dishes. You can try everything from French crêpes and American pancakes to traditional Arabic sweets and fresh fruit juices. There is also a special children’s pavilion where we can participate in a live cooking competition. If we enter, we will have to create a unique dessert using a basket of mystery ingredients!

Aside from the food, there are glowing lanterns hanging from the trees, live music, and puppet shows. It gets incredibly busy in the evening, so I suggest we meet at the main gate at 2:00 PM on Saturday before the huge crowds arrive. Entrance is completely free, but you will need some pocket money to buy food items. Let me know if your parents say yes!

Your friend,
Mona
