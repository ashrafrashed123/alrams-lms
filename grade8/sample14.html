National Festivals
National festivals are important because they bring people together and help them remember their history. In many countries, a national festival includes music, traditional clothes, food, speeches and bright decorations. Families often travel to public squares or parks, where they watch shows and join community activities. These events are not only for entertainment; they also teach young people about values such as respect, unity and pride. During some festivals, students prepare projects about famous leaders, old buildings or important events from the past. Volunteers may help by guiding visitors, cleaning the area or giving information to tourists. Food is usually an important part of the celebration because it allows families to share recipes and welcome guests. However, large crowds can create problems if people leave rubbish or ignore safety rules. For this reason, organisers often use signs, police officers and first-aid teams to keep visitors safe. Some festivals also use online tickets to control the number of people entering busy areas. A successful national festival is colourful and enjoyable, but it is also well organised. It reminds people that celebrating a country is about more than flags and songs; it is about understanding what connects the community and protecting shared traditions.

My Travel Blog: Africa
Last winter, I travelled with my uncle across part of East Africa, and I wrote this blog to remember the journey. We began in a busy city near the coast, where markets were full of fruit, spices and handmade bags. The next morning, we joined a small group and drove inland. At first, the road was smooth, but later it became narrow and dusty. Our guide told us to keep our cameras ready because animals sometimes crossed the road. In the afternoon, we saw giraffes eating leaves from tall trees and elephants walking slowly beside a river. I was amazed by the quiet power of the animals and the huge space around us. On the third day, we visited a village where local people showed us how they saved water during dry months. Their simple tools were clever and useful. I realised that travelling is not only about seeing famous places; it is also about learning from people who live differently. The hardest part was the heat, especially when our bus stopped for repairs. Still, everyone stayed patient and shared water. By the end of the trip, I felt more confident, more curious and much more grateful for the natural world.

At the Children's Food Festival
When I arrived at the Children's Food Festival, the park was already shining with coloured lanterns. My friend Omar had invited me because his class was performing a short play about healthy sweets from different countries. At the entrance, volunteers gave each visitor a small map and a card for collecting stamps. First, we followed the smell of pancakes to a bright yellow tent. A chef showed children how to mix flour, milk and dates instead of too much sugar. Next, we watched Omar's play near the main stage. The actors were nervous at the beginning, but the crowd clapped loudly, and they soon spoke with confidence. After that, we visited a stall where children decorated paper lanterns and wrote messages about friendship. I wanted to buy every sweet I saw, but I remembered that the festival was also teaching balance and good choices. Before we left, Omar and I used our completed stamp cards to vote for the best activity. I chose the cooking tent because it was practical, enjoyable and connected food with culture. The festival ended with music, and families walked home smiling, carrying recipes, paper lanterns and colourful memories from every tent and friendly food stall.
