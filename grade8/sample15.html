Part 3A: Reading – Eid Al Adha (24 Marks)

Towards Grade Level Goal  |  Expository text (A2.1–A2.2)

Read the text. Choose the correct answer. Choose True, False or Not Given for each statement.

Eid Al Adha is one of the most important festivals in the Islamic calendar. It is celebrated every year to remember the story of Prophet Ibrahim, who showed his complete faith and obedience to God. The festival lasts for three days and takes place at the end of the Hajj pilgrimage season, when Muslims from all over the world travel to Mecca in Saudi Arabia.

On the morning of Eid Al Adha, Muslims wake up early and wear their best clothes before going to perform a special prayer at the mosque. After the prayer, families gather together to share large and delicious meals, which often include meat, rice, and traditional sweet dishes.

One of the most significant traditions during Eid Al Adha is the act of animal sacrifice. A sheep, goat, or cow is sacrificed, and the meat is then divided into three equal parts: one for the family, one for friends and neighbours, and one for people who are in need. This important tradition teaches the values of sharing and generosity.

In the UAE, Eid Al Adha is an official public holiday. Schools, universities, and government offices close for several days, giving families time to visit relatives and travel. Many shopping centres and public parks organise special activities and entertainment for children and families during the holiday period.

4. Most Emirati families spend Eid Al Adha travelling abroad. [4 marks]

At Grade Level Goal  |  Narrative text – Blog Post (A2.2–B1.1)

Read the text and answer the questions. Choose A, B or C.

My Journey to Morocco  |  By Emma Clarke  |  Travel & Exploration Blog
Last summer, I had the most incredible adventure of my life when I travelled to Morocco for two weeks. It was my first time visiting Africa, and the experience completely changed the way I see the world.

I arrived in Marrakech, a city famous for its ancient markets called souks. As I was walking through the narrow winding streets of the old city, I was completely amazed by the noise, colours, and smells all around me. Sellers were calling out to customers, musicians were playing traditional instruments, and the air was filled with the sweet scent of spices and fresh mint tea.

One afternoon, I hired a local guide named Youssef to take me to the Atlas Mountains. The drive through the mountain roads was far more breathtaking than I had ever imagined. Youssef shared fascinating stories about the Berber people, who have lived in those mountains for thousands of years. He explained that the word ‘Berber’ has been used by outsiders for centuries, but that these communities prefer to call themselves ‘Amazigh’, a word which means ‘free people’.

Travelling through Morocco taught me that the very best way to truly understand a culture is to speak with the people who live and breathe it every single day.

Subject: The Lantern Festival – You Must Come!  |  From: Rania  |  To: Hana
Hi Hana,

I just had to write and tell you about something truly unforgettable! Last weekend, my family and I attended the Spring Lantern Festival at the Cultural Village in Abu Dhabi, and it was honestly one of the most beautiful experiences I have ever had.

The festival celebrates the end of the Chinese New Year season. Hundreds of enormous, brightly coloured lanterns were displayed all around the outdoor park, each one painted with different animals, flowers, and traditional Chinese symbols. The atmosphere was absolutely magical – the lanterns glowed in deep reds, warm golds, and vivid greens against the dark evening sky.

Throughout the evening there were also live performances of traditional Chinese music and dance. I particularly loved watching a group of young children performing a dragon dance, which is traditionally believed to bring good luck and happiness for the year ahead. I must admit that when the performers moved the long dragon through the crowd, I felt completely mesmerised – I simply could not take my eyes off it.

What I found most meaningful was learning that the lantern festival symbolises letting go of past difficulties and welcoming new beginnings with hope and positivity. I left feeling genuinely inspired.

I believe the festival is planned again for next spring. I strongly recommend that you go – it is absolutely worth it!

Rania
