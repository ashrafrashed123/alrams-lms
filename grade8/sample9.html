Reading 1: My First Day in Gabon

14 marks · Multiple Choice (A, B, C) · 2 marks each

Last summer, I travelled to Gabon with my uncle, who writes articles about rivers and forests. I had never been to Central Africa before, so I felt excited and nervous at the same time. We arrived on the west coast early in the morning. The air was warm, and the port was already busy with fishermen, workers and small boats.

At first, we travelled by boat along the Ogouée River. I was taking photos when the boat suddenly slowed down. The river became narrower, so our guide told us to change to a canoe. I was worried because the canoe looked very small, but the guide smiled and said, "This is the best way to continue."

While we were moving up the river, I saw colourful birds, tall trees and children waving from the riverbank. Later, we stopped near a village to collect information for my uncle's article. The people were friendly and told us about fishing, farming and life beside the river.

That evening, I wrote in my diary. I was tired, but I felt proud because I had tried something new. It was not just a trip; it was a real expedition.

Reading 2: Across the River

14 marks · Multiple Choice (A, B, C) · 2 marks each

Yesterday was the most exciting day of my journey in Africa. I woke up before sunrise because our guide, Musa, wanted us to leave the camp early. We were travelling to a small village near the river. Musa said the journey was long, but the views were magnificent.

First, we walked through a forest path. The ground was wet because it had rained during the night. While we were walking, I heard birds calling loudly above us. I wanted to stop every few minutes to take pictures, but Musa told us to keep moving.

After two hours, we reached a small port. There were no large ships there, only wooden boats and two canoes. We climbed into one of the canoes and crossed the river slowly. I felt nervous when the canoe moved from side to side, but Musa was calm. He had crossed the river many times before.

When we arrived at the village, some children ran to meet us. Their teacher invited us into the school and showed us a map of the area. I learned that many families use the river every day for travel, fishing and carrying goods. By the evening, I understood that rivers are like roads in this part of Africa.

Reading 3: A Blog from the Coast

14 marks · Multiple Choice (A, B, C) · 2 marks each

I am writing this blog from a small guesthouse near the coast of Tanzania. My journey began three days ago when I arrived in Dar es Salaam. I planned to visit a nearby island and learn about life by the sea. I thought the journey would be easy, but I was wrong.

On the first morning, I went to the port with my backpack and notebook. Many people were waiting for boats. Some were carrying fruit, while others were carrying boxes and bags. While I was looking for my guide, a young man called Hamisi came over and asked my name. He was my guide for the day.

We travelled by boat across the blue water. The sea was calm at first, but after thirty minutes the wind became stronger. I held my notebook tightly because I was afraid it would fall into the water. Hamisi laughed and told me not to worry.

When we reached the island, I met a fisherman who showed me how he fixed his nets. He said the sea gives people food, work and transport. Before this trip, I thought the coast was only beautiful. Now I know it is also very important.

Reading 4: Lost on the Forest Path

14 marks · Multiple Choice (A, B, C) · 2 marks each

Last month, I joined a school expedition to a forest area in Ghana. Our teachers wanted us to learn how explorers use maps and careful planning. Before we left, they told us that every group must stay together and follow the guide.

At the beginning, everything was easy. We were walking slowly, taking notes about plants and listening to birds. I was working with my friend Sami. He was drawing a map while I was writing the names of the trees. After lunch, the path became less clear. Tall grass covered the ground, and we could not see the guide for a few minutes.

Sami thought we should continue, but I remembered the teacher's rule. I told him we must stop and wait. While we were waiting, we heard our guide calling our names. He was not far away, but he was worried because we had left the main path.

The guide said we made the right choice by stopping. He explained that explorers must be brave, but they must also be careful. That evening, I wrote in my blog that the most important tool in an expedition is not always a map. Sometimes, it is good judgement.

Reading 5: Following Mary Kingsley's Route

14 marks · Multiple Choice (A, B, C) · 2 marks each

This week, our class project was about famous travellers in Africa. I chose Mary Kingsley because her journey sounded brave and interesting. In 1895, she travelled to the west coast of Africa and went up the Ogouée River in Gabon. She wanted to study how people lived and to collect fish.

For my project, I did not travel as far as Mary Kingsley, but I visited a small river area with my father. I wanted to imagine what her journey felt like. We started at a small port and travelled by boat. While we were moving along the river, I thought about how difficult travelling was in the past. There were no mobile phones, fast engines or easy maps.

Later, we changed to a canoe because the water became shallow. I felt excited because this was similar to Mary Kingsley's journey. We saw fishermen collecting fish and children playing near the river. I took notes and photos for my school presentation.

When I got home, I understood why explorers wrote books about their travels. A journey can teach us more than facts. It can help us understand people, places and history in a deeper way.
