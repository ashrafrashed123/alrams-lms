Part 3A: Reading – True / False / Not Given (24 marks)

National festivals are important because they help people remember history, share traditions, and feel part of one community. In many countries, a national festival includes flags, music, food, and public events. Families may travel to a city centre or gather in local parks to watch shows and fireworks. Schools often prepare short performances, art displays, and projects about famous people or important events. These activities make learning more meaningful because students connect facts with real experiences. Some festivals also welcome visitors from other countries. They can taste local food, listen to traditional songs, and learn polite ways to join the celebration. However, large crowds can create problems. Streets become busy, rubbish may increase, and some people may forget safety rules. For this reason, organisers plan carefully before the festival begins. They provide signs, water stations, first-aid teams, and special areas for families. A successful national festival is not only bright and enjoyable; it also teaches respect, responsibility, and pride in culture. When young people understand why a festival matters, they usually behave better and invite others to join in a thoughtful way. Therefore, festivals can protect the past while bringing different generations together in the present, especially when everyone follows clear rules.

Part 3B: Reading – Multiple Choice (28 marks)

Last winter, my uncle and I travelled across part of Africa by bus and train. I wrote this blog because the journey changed the way I think about travel. On the first day, we crossed a dry area where the land looked empty from far away. When we stopped, I noticed small plants, birds, and people selling water near the road. Later, we visited a busy market beside a river. The colours, sounds, and smells were stronger than anything I had seen before. My uncle told me to take photos, but also to ask before photographing people. The hardest part was a long train ride at night. It was crowded and noisy, so sleeping was almost impossible. However, I met a student who was going home after exams. He explained which places were safe to visit and which roads were closed after heavy rain. By the end of the trip, I understood that expeditions are not just about distance. They are about patience, respect, and learning from people you meet. I also learned that a good traveller should not rush from one famous place to another. Listening to local advice, noticing small details, and accepting delays can make a journey safer and more meaningful.
